---
Title: "I will walk away from your interview process"
Date: "2026-07-06"
---

Getting paid to write software has been a weird thing for the past couple of decades. You learned the trade to the best of your possibilities, joined a network, rolled the dice in the market and eventually got an offer. If you were amongst the most disciplined you could even get chased out by companies, become the company business or the company itself. Most of the well compensated software engineers operating nowadays remain being either of that kind or of the kind of those that come out of elite formal education.

Up until recently writing software was a niche occupation. The hard skills required dedication, brain power, time and willingness to endure long hours in front of a computer screen. Over time, a massive demand for software products motivated by the advent of personal computers and then by the Internet and smartphones turned programmers into a scarce and ridiculously valued resource. For decades devaluing money consistently transferred from the pockets of people at the bottom of the labour pyramid poured into whatever could become software. Computer programs carried the promise of efficiency, convenience, more fun and perpetual connection.

But programmers were most of the time seen by companies as every other laborer. Resources. Numbers on a spreadsheet enduring dehumanization in exchange for a sure and safe passage to a more humane and comfortable future. Alongside financing and marketing, theres really no other profession I can think of that is more alienating than the profession of the average software developer. And in this soup there is something particularly special about being a programmer which is how technology becomes part of the worker identity.

The recent rise of LLMs and semi-autonomous intelligent systems gave way for a radical transformation in the landscape of the software business. We're still in its infancy but the consequences are already being felt. A dread economical and political landscape across the world is also contributing to the current state of things. Money seems to be flowing everywhere against the interest of the regular citizen. Job security has been under attack for several years now and is currently at an all time high in the western world since the mid 20th century. In essence working a secure job in the private sector became a privilege now more than ever.

In this conundrum software companies resist the change and keep playing by the same rules of the pre-pandemic years. They insist on the same hiring strategies that worked five years ago ignoring the depth in which recent events are bound to have in their business and the humanity in general within the next few years. For most of the business where software development is core, multi-step processes full of coding puzzles and whiteboard challenges are set to fail. They've been a target for heated arguments throughout the past decade, a discussion that is a about to settle by the facts of reality.

The effective modern developer won't be the one that burns through and memorize solutions to achieve a high score. A good software engineer was never that. Being able to articulate a problem in a conversation with a well trained AI system that lands on a solution that integrates well with production systems is the skill to look for now. Again, as I write this piece these events take shape. Everything is brand new and demand a well informed approach. There are pre-conditions business need to accommodate for in order to be able to move effectively. For now most companies seem to be lost or appear to be too stiff to be able to assimilate the light-speed changes taking shape.

Truth to be said the fundamental aspects of a successful strategy for creating and managing software products are as old as the machines created to run them. The news are the speed in which the margins for bloat and non-sense are shrinking. At the dawn of the AI era I really hope that businesses wake up and make the intentional effort to put people and human interactions at the center of their decisions.
